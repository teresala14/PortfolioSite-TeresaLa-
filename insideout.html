Inside Out

Created to make it easier for users to track their nutrition and exercise while giving them all the resources they need to live a healthier lifestyle.

Problem

Young professionals of various backgrounds struggle to eat healthily and find time to exercise due to their hectic schedules. Work-life imbalance has become the norm and a constant struggle for young adults. This results in their health taking a backseat to other priorities.

Solution

Inside Out wants to make taking care of your health easier and more accessible for people with no time to constantly research healthy recipes or find workouts that work for them. Users will be able find a substantial database of healthy recipes and versatile workouts. Users can easily log their nutrition and workouts as well as set health goals without hindering their busy schedules.

Research

Interviews

During the research portion of my project, I conducted three interviews with users ranging from late 20s-early 30s. The objective is to determine the how much they value a healthy lifestyle, what kind of healthy habits they currently have, and goals and struggles in regard to their personal health. I used the findings to identify user needs and pain points as well as identify the current digital resources they are using to narrow the scope of the project.

Insights gathered from the research were arranged into an affinity map highlighting key findings and solutions

Surveys

I conducted a survey via Google Forms with a total of 14 responses to better understand user needs and their current healthy habits. The results confirmed that while users care about improving their health, they lack the time to integrate those habits into their daily life.

Analyze

Personas

In order to better understand user goals and create a tailored experience for them, I created three personas with the insights gathered from the interviews and survey. Each persona utilizes a specific identity with different needs.

When deciding the different metrics of workouts and recipes I would reflect back on these personas:

Michael (The Gym Bro): He is looking for more protein intake and a structured routine to follow.

Vanessa (The Working Woman): She is looking to get back in shape, but her busy schedule makes it difficult for her.

Laura (The Influencer): She likes variety in her exercises and fad diets and doesn't like to follow a boring routine.

User Journey and User Flows

I created three user journeys for each persona to map out their emotions, pain points, and steps they would need to take to meet their success criteria.

From this point, I was able to identify each point in their task flow and begin to identify possible drop-off points as well as opportunities to improve the overall experience and determine what features to add to the final experience.

Notable Opportunity: Adding star rating for recipes

Notable Opportunity: Adding separate page for weekly reminders

Notable Opportunity: Manual-add for food items that are not searchable

Then, I created task flow for each persona to map each step from their entry point to their success criteria.

Card Sorting

To create the information architecture, I conducted 5 remote card-sorting sessions for 4 categories with a total of 28 cards. Most categories were accurately placed into the expected category with a few exceptions: "Learn" category was renamed to "Read" because during several sessions "FAQ" was sorted into "Learn" rather than "Settings."

Sitemap: The app was laid into 4 different sections: Move, Read, Eat, Home. Move would be the exercise-focused page while Eat would be the nutrition-focused page. I laid both pages out with similar functions to display equal importance and hierarchy.

Ideation

Sketches

I began to sketch my ideas to lay out my design based on all the research collected. In order to make the dashboard to be easily accessible to the nutrition and activity summary, the user can immediately log information without unnecessary navigation. Each main function will have a search bar to reduce extra scrolling and friction for the user to meet the success criteria.

Task flows were sketched first to ensure I was hitting every step in the flow before adding more detail to the design to limit errors in later iterations.

Task Flow: Adding recipe to nutrition log

Wireframes

Using Figma, I translated my sketches into mid-fidelity wireframes. In these wireframes, the main functions of logging a recipe and logging workout were fully laid out.There were two possible versions of the Eat and Move page that I designed, which would later be finalized after testing.

Testing

Usability Testing

After creating a fully functional clickable prototype, 6 usability tests were conducted with participants ranging from their 20s-30s with knowledge of how to use mobile apps. The goal of this study is to measure how difficult the users find accomplishing tasks using the main features of the app. This round of testing was to minimize any friction and major functionality user may have with the product. The tasks observed were:

  • Observe the ease with which the user can navigate the main functions of the app.
  • Determine if the user can successfully add an item to the nutrition log.
  • Determine if the user can successfully log a workout into their activity log.
  • Determine if the user can successfully complete the onboarding process.

Usability Test Results: Errors ranked on severity from 1-5

Issue 1

All participants struggled to go back from the nutrition log. (high severity)

Suggested Change: Add a back button to easily return to the Eat Page and a secondary button in order to easily close the log and return to the log screen.

A secondary test showed no further issues with the navigation from this page

Issue 2

When logging a workout or a food item, participants noted that there was no way to undo the action once the button was pressed. (High Severity)

Suggested Change: Updated dialogue box will be a CTA to either confirm or undo the action.

Issue 3

Participants found the "Eat" page overcrowded and difficult to navigate. When searching for a specific button, it took much longer for them than expected, signifying that the cognitive load of the page was too high.

Suggested Change: The layout of the page was changed to the same format as the "Move" page increasing cohesiveness of the overall design.

This was further tested through a preference test where 67% of participants preferred Design B.

Implementation

Visual Design

After testing, I proceeded to create the final elements of the design. I followed the Material Design guidelines and designed for the iPhone X, 12, 13, and 14. After going through several iterations of color, design, and formatting before settling on the final version. The final iteration was a clean style without too many elements. I decided to use predominately white backgrounds to make the screens look cleaner and minimalistic. For the logs and cards, I leaned towards a slight neumorphic style for a modern look

Final Iteration: Dashboard, Move, Eat, Read, Nutrition and Activity Log

Design System

I created a design system with the typography, iconography, color palette and broke down various UI elements to improve handoff to developers. The element coloring coordinates with its corresponding page: Move (blue), Eat (Green), Read (purple) for a cohesive and intuitive layout.

Evaluate

Next Steps

After a round of testing, I was able to fix major functionality issues and step in the direction of what I thought users would enjoy in terms of design. In the future steps, I would like to further test visual preferences, new functions, and visit other possible improvements.

1. Accessibility

I would like to add more accessibility to people with specific diet preferences and physical disabilities. I'd like to have a wide database to accommodate various user needs and paint points.

2. Upgrade Plans

I would like to add an upgrade plan to allow users to access exclusive resources/workouts and tailored nutrition plans, so they can more easily reach their goals.

3. Experts

Allow users to be able to talk to nutritionists and trainers to get their feedback on their current routine. An addition of a group of experts would increase overall trust in the product.

Learnings

As this is my first project, I have learned so much about the design process. From conducting research, creating personas, usability testing, and refining the design, the experience from this project has vastly improved my knowledge for creating a user-centric experience.
